How to Read This Report
🏛️
Party Bands
Blue shading = Democratic president, Red = Republican, Gray = split government. Congressional control shown as a separate stripe below the chart.
⚡
Event Markers
Toggle Economic Shocks (amber), Legislation (green), and Fed Policy (purple) on/off. Hover any marker for full details on the event and its measured impact.
📐
Scoring Method
Scores are percentile ranks (0–100) vs. all administrations since 1950. Hover any score to see the Z-score (standard deviations from average) for statistical depth.
⏱️
Lag Attribution
Presidents are credited for outcomes 6–12 months after taking office. Congress: 12–18 months. Legislation windows show pre/immediate/long-term impact separately.
🔀
Unified Gov Flag
A bold border highlights periods when one party controlled the White House + both chambers simultaneously — isolating full policy mandate periods.
📑
3-Tier System
Tier 1 (Core) carries the most scoring weight. Tier 2 (Supporting) adds nuance. Tier 3 (Context) is reference only — it tracks external forces no administration controls.

Note on Tier 3 scoring: These metrics are intentionally excluded from party scoring. The Federal Funds Rate reflects independent Fed decisions; oil prices are globally determined; markets respond to global capital flows. Total Public Debt is the accumulated stock of all prior deficits — inherited by every administration — so it is shown here as context rather than a scored outcome. They appear here to provide essential interpretive context.

Methodology: Scores are percentile ranks (0th–100th) calculated against all administrations 1950–2024. Presidential outcomes are lagged 6–12 months from inauguration. Congressional outcomes are lagged 12–18 months from the start of each Congress. For metrics where lower is better (unemployment, inflation, deficit, poverty, Gini), the percentile is inverted so that 100 always = best performance. Tier 1 carries 50% of composite weight; Tier 2 carries 50%. Hover any score cell to see the Z-score (standard deviations from historical mean).

Key Caveats
Economic outcomes are multi-causal. No statistical model can fully isolate presidential or congressional impact from global forces, inherited conditions, or Federal Reserve decisions. These scores are a structured framework for comparison — not a definitive causal claim. Use the Context tab to understand the external environment each administration faced.
